Book: The Bitcoin Standard

Jan 19, 2019 books
3 min read

Overview

The book is logically split into two parts.

Chapters 1-7: The author examines the various forms of money and its properties. They then review the economic systems and money standards employed throughout history, as well as the impact each that had on societies and their productivity. The author spends a fair bit of this part criticising Keynes and his economics which currently remain widely taught in academia. The criticism seemed repetitive at times and would have profited from being more abridged, still the influx of new economic knowledge - especially for someone without such a background - was appreciable and pleasing.

Chapters 8-10: In this part, the author introduces Bitcoin and discusses the motivation behind its creation; they detail its fixed supply rate and the technology underlying its functioning - blockchain - and how the technology obviates the need for trusted third-parties overlooking transactions. The author did well to discuss money in general and the productivity of various economies in the earlier part, as that adequately laid the groundwork for the discussion in this part on Bitcoin’s contribution to current economies.

One contentious point of the author’s is that blockchain technology is only applicable in digital-cash systems and has no role to play in other industries and no other use-cases. Personally, I disagree. Sure, many of the altcoins created a few years back did not provide grand utility and were quick ways to raise capital from hasty investors. But since then, billions have been poured in to the sector with the aim of researching and improving blockchain to further many industries and solve long-existing problems.
